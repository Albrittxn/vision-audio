NARRATIVE_DATE: 2027-03-19
DAY_TYPE: travel
DAY_LABEL: Singapore (with Karen and Jasmine)

The stale, recirculated air of the cabin is the first thing that hits me. It’s a familiar weight, a faint metallic tang that always accompanies long-haul flights. Today is Friday, March 19, 2027. We’re somewhere over the Pacific, roughly halfway into our Singapore trip. This is our arrival day. Jasmine will be six months and seventeen days old today.

Karen is asleep beside me, her head nestled into the neck pillow, her balayage hair falling across her face. The heart pendant she always wears rises and falls with her breath. Jasmine, cocooned in her bassinet attachment in front of us, is stirring. A soft coo, then a louder gurgle. The low thrum of the aircraft engines is a constant, almost hypnotic hum.

I shift in my seat, the leather creaking softly. Restless. My internal clock is already at war with the time zones. We left Vegas yesterday afternoon, the Z06 parked securely at the airport, the Mercedes taking us to the terminal with Jasmine’s car seat firmly buckled. Now, hours later, the distant light filtering through the small window port is a deep, bruised purple, indicating some version of dawn.

My mind cycles through the planned itinerary: the meetings for Entheios AI’s expansion into the APAC market, the potential partners at Compass and Keller Williams Asia, the dinners Karen and I have penciled in. Singapore is a target. The precision of the city appeals to me.

Jasmine’s cooing grows louder, a happy, inquisitive sound. She’s starting to push herself up, those small, powerful arms working hard. When I lean over, her big dark eyes lock onto mine, and a wide, gummy smile splits her face, all dimples and infant joy. “Dada!” she babbles, a string of pure sound without meaning, but the intent is clear. She’s recognized me. I run a finger over her soft, dark hair, already showing hints of my own coloring blending with Karen’s warmer tones.

Karen stretches, a soft groan escaping her. Her eyes flutter open, dark brown and still hazy with sleep. “Morning,” she murmurs, her voice thick. She sees Jasmine. “Hey, Jazzy-girl.” She takes Jasmine from the bassinet, pulling her close. Jasmine immediately starts mouthing at Karen’s shoulder, a constant stream of drool already soaking into the fabric of Karen’s travel top. Six months, and everything goes in the mouth.

“Rough night?” I ask, my voice low.

Karen shakes her head, gently wiping Jasmine’s chin. “Just adjusting. This is what, 4 AM Vegas time?”

“Something like that.” I glance at my Cartier Santos, the time set to local Singapore time. 7:15 AM. The crew will be bringing breakfast soon. The thought of airline food does nothing for my appetite.

We manage to get Jasmine fed and changed in the cramped lavatory, a delicate dance of coordination. She’s getting stronger, wriggling, trying to grab at everything. Her independent sitting for short stretches is impressive, but not enough for the overhead. Karen’s patience is a slow, steady current. Mine is less so, a tighter rein.

By the time we’re back in our seats, tray tables down, the stewardess is asking for our breakfast order. I choose something simple: eggs, fruit, coffee. The smell of the coffee, weak and institutional, fills the small space.

As I sip the lukewarm coffee, my eyes scan the cabin. And then I see him. Two rows up, across the aisle. Chad. Chad Miller. A flicker of recognition, then the mental catalog flips back to early college, to a time when I was chasing different things. He was a flake then, a guy who talked big, delivered small. Always had an angle, never a foundation. I remember a particularly botched attempt at a dropshipping venture, years ago. He owed me a couple hundred dollars that he just ghosted on. Small money, but the principle stuck.

He’s talking animatedly to the man next to him, gesturing with a hand that wears a thick, gold wedding band. His suit, though slightly rumpled from the flight, is clearly expensive, a subtle pattern in the weave. His hair is neatly cut, not the shaggy mess I remember. He looks… solid. Like he built something.

I watch him for a moment longer, a faint curiosity stirring. People change. Sometimes. It's a low surprise, not a shock. The world is a big place, but also small. I realize he’s running a company now, something in logistics, I think I vaguely heard. Doing well. Good for him. The thought passes. No days off. Not for me.

Karen nudges me. “What’s up?”

“Just someone I used to know,” I say, keeping my voice neutral. I don’t elaborate. The past is a closed book.

Jasmine, propped against Karen’s chest, lets out a delighted laugh, a full-bellied sound that makes a few heads turn. She’s reaching for a plastic cup on the tray table, her pincer grasp emerging, those tiny fingers trying to pinch the rim. Karen lets her play with it, making soft cooing sounds.

The light outside intensifies, a blinding white now, reflecting off the vast expanse of clouds. My thoughts drift to the team back in Vegas, the numbers I reviewed before we left. Entheios AI is running smooth. The over $50k/month in profit is not just revenue; it's the quiet hum of an engine performing exactly as designed. My role is strategic, not operational. I architect. I review. I make the big calls.

My phone, in airplane mode, sits face down on the tray table. The notifications will flood in once we land, a cascade of updates, requests, confirmations. I’ll address them, precisely, deliberately. But for now, the flight sim sessions I’ve been logging are a better parallel for this state – a suspended reality, everything pre-programmed, the variables contained.

I close my eyes for a moment, letting the sound of the engines and Jasmine’s soft babbling wash over me. The restlessness persists, a low buzz in my nerves, a desire to be on the ground, moving. I don’t sit still well. But the forced stillness of the flight is also an exercise in control. Patience.

When my eyes open again, Chad is looking directly at me. He’s seen me. A flash of recognition, then a slightly awkward half-smile. I give him a short, almost imperceptible nod. No more, no less. He turns back to his conversation, a quick, almost imperceptible shift of discomfort in his posture. Good.

Karen’s hand finds mine under the blanket, a silent connection. Her skin is warm. Jasmine starts to fuss, her babbling turning into a whimper. Karen starts to bounce her gently, murmuring soft words in Spanish. “Cariño, mi amor.”

The captain’s voice crackles over the intercom, announcing our descent. Singapore. The air outside the window changes, thickens. I can almost smell the humidity, the rich, unfamiliar scents of a new city. The prospect of fresh air, of concrete under my feet, is a small relief.

Soon, we’ll be on the ground. Through immigration, to the hotel, the Plaza Athénée, if the bookings went through without issue. Then, a quick reset, a change of clothes, and the city will open up. The strategic meetings, the dinners, the careful expansion of Entheios AI. The Z06 is thousands of miles away, but the principle is the same. The grind. No days off.

Jasmine’s whimper fades into a sleepy sigh. Karen is humming, her voice a soft, low melody. The aircraft banks gently, and through the window, I catch a glimpse of green, a hint of concrete structures. The light is a full, vibrant tropical yellow now.

The weight of the dog chain necklace against my sternum feels heavier, more present. The journey continues. The plan remains constant. I look at Karen, her face serene, then at Jasmine, her small chest rising and falling in sleep. This is what it’s for. The quiet presence. The deliberate moves. I know I’m the man now.
