The cool air of the morning bites a little, even indoors. It’s Tuesday, March 2, 2027. Winter’s last gasp, though the desert sun will burn it off soon enough. The HVAC hums a low, consistent note, a blanket over the silence. My phone lies face down on the nightstand, its screen dark. I don't need it yet. Water first. The glass is already on my side of the bed, condensation beading on the cool surface. I drain it in two long gulps.

Karen stirs beside me, a soft sigh escaping her lips. Her balayage hair, dark at the roots, fans across the pillow. I watch her for a beat, the rise and fall of her chest a steady rhythm. Jasmine isn’t stirring yet, which means a few more minutes of this quiet, this stillness before the day truly begins. I roll out of bed, the carpet soft underfoot. The faint scent of her perfume, a warm vanilla, lingers in the air.

The coffee machine whirs to life in the kitchen. Not the espresso machine today. Drip. A softer start. Karen prefers it that way on Tuesdays, and I’m good with it. While it brews, I move through the simple motions: splash cold water on my face, feel the skin tighten. My reflection in the bathroom mirror is sharp. Defined. The jawline, the cut in my shoulders even before the gym. It’s all there. The necklace, the dog chain from TG, rests against my sternum, cool and heavy.

A faint gurgle from the nursery. Jasmine. Six months old today. March 2nd. She’s starting to really sit up now, her balance improving daily. I can hear her babbling, a string of "babababa"s, consonants making their appearance. Karen is still asleep. I pad down the hall, the hardwood cool beneath my bare feet. The nursery is dim, soft greens and creams absorbing the early light. Jasmine is upright in her crib, a wide, gummy smile spreading across her face as she sees me. She reaches out with both hands, an insistent grasp, her fingers opening and closing. I lift her, her weight solid and warm against my chest. She coos, then buries her face into my shoulder, a little sigh escaping her.

I carry her back to the kitchen, the smell of brewing coffee filling the air. Karen is awake now, leaning against the counter, stretching, a sleepy smile for both of us. “Morning, Papa Bear,” she mumbles, reaching for her mug. Her cropped set is a soft grey, comfortable for a home day. Jasmine lets out a squeal of delight, reaching for the steam rising from Karen’s coffee. Karen gently pulls her back, a familiar laugh escaping her.

At Lifetime Summerlin, the gym is already a low thrum of activity. The air smells of rubber and disinfectant. I rack the bar, 225 lbs already loaded. Bench day. Headphones on, the rap starts, hard and driving. Central Cee, then Lil Baby. My breath is already catching. I push through the first set, the steel cool in my hands, the weight heavy and familiar on my chest. This isn't about getting bigger anymore. It's about maintaining, about the discipline. For the mind.

The frustration starts subtly, a quiet hum beneath the surface of my focus. I’m deep into the second set, pushing the rep count, when the music cuts out. Not a pause, but a complete stop. Dead silence in my ears, save for the distant clatter of plates and the grunts of other lifters. My internal rhythm, the one that drives the lift, shatters. I rack the bar, a little harder than necessary. I pull out my phone, swipe, restart the playlist. It’s a minor thing, a glitch in the carefully constructed routine. But it’s enough to catch. Enough to niggle.

Back home, the house smells faintly clean, a testament to the reed diffusers and the Roomba that navigates silently through the common spaces. My office, with its panoramic view of the Vegas valley, is bathed in a pale morning light. The ultrawide monitor glows, a single email client open. I sit at the solid wood desk, Mac mini tucked neatly out of sight. No clutter. Just the keyboard and mouse. Two hours of deep work, and the hum of frustration from the gym lingers, an unwelcome static. The numbers for Entheios are good, steady. Over $50k/month in profit. Boring in the best way. But my focus feels fragmented, not sharp. I make calls, review pipelines. The team is running, but I keep finding myself second-guessing. A client success report feels light. A new lead gen strategy could be more aggressive. It's not a crisis, but it’s a nagging feeling that things aren’t *perfect*.

Karen walks in around 10:30, a soft knock on the open door. Jasmine is balanced on her hip, holding onto Karen’s shirt, mouth open in a little 'o'. Karen’s smile is easy. "Taking a break, locked in?" she asks, a playful lilt in her voice. I nod, push back from the desk. I take Jasmine from her, the warmth of her small body a grounding force. She points a chubby finger at the monitor, babbling, "Bah-bah-bah." I bounce her gently. Karen leans in, a quick kiss on my cheek. "Lunch soon," she says, then she’s gone, leaving the faint scent of her perfume in the air.

The afternoon holds a MacDonald Highlands property tour. A real estate agent, one of Entheios’s key Compass clients, needs a new primary residence. He reached out to me directly, a rare request, for an opinion. Not for my expertise in real estate, but for my eye. He trusts my judgment, the quiet precision I bring. The Z06 purrs low as I pull out of the Summerlin gates. Gloss black, no frills, just muscle. It’s early March, so the air is still crisp, but the sun beats down with its usual Vegas intensity. I press the AC, just a touch.

The drive is short. MacDonald Highlands rises in the distance, a bastion of quiet wealth against the rugged Red Rock. I put on something smoother, a bit of Bruno Mars, the bass a soft pulse. The sense of low-grade frustration persists, an echo of the morning. It makes the act of driving, of being in motion, more acute. The Z06 isn’t just a car; it’s a release, a moment of pure focus on the road ahead.

The house itself is a sprawling modern, all clean lines and vast windows. It smells of fresh paint and new tile, that particular scent of untouched luxury. The agent, a guy named David, greets me at the door. He’s wearing a crisp suit, his energy a little too effusive. “Ryan, thanks for coming out, man. Really appreciate your eye on this.” I nod, my expression neutral. My watch, a Cartier Santos, catches the light. David starts his pitch, talking about square footage and custom cabinetry. I walk through the rooms, deliberate, letting my presence pull the attention. I don’t speak much. I just observe. The kitchen, with its induction range, feels cold. The primary suite, though massive, lacks warmth. The view, however, is undeniable – the entire Vegas valley spread out below, a glittering tapestry.

David is talking about potential. “Think of the gatherings you could have here, the clients you could host…” He gestures expansively. I stop in the living area, by a floor-to-ceiling window. The setting sun is starting its descent, painting the sky in soft oranges and purples. It’s beautiful. But the house itself feels… hollow. Too much space. Too little soul.

“It’s not for him,” I say, my voice low, cutting through David’s monologue. He stops, mid-sentence. His smile falters, just for a second. “Too much noise. It’ll feel empty after a month.”

David blinks. “Empty?”

“He needs something with more anchor,” I clarify. “More intimacy. This is a display. Not a home.” I turn from the window, my gaze steady on David’s. “It’s a good house. But it’s not his house.”

He takes it in, processes it. The frustration in my own gut finds an unexpected outlet in this clarity. It feels good to cut through the fluff, to deliver the truth without apology. David nods slowly. “Okay. Okay, I hear you. Thanks, Ryan.” He means it. He knows I'm not playing games.

The Z06 winds back down the hill. The sun is a blazing orange now, melting into the distant Spring Mountains. I keep the music off, the silence broken only by the low rumble of the V8. Karen sends a text: *Steak tonight?* A small smile touches my lips. *Perfect.*

Back in Summerlin, the house is a warm beacon. Karen is in the kitchen, prepping dinner. The smell of garlic and olive oil is already in the air. Jasmine is in her high chair, pushing a bit of puréed sweet potato around her tray with a tiny hand. She looks up, sees me, and lets out a delighted gurgle, grabbing at the air. Her babbling is a constant backdrop to our evenings now.

Dinner is simple: steak, perfectly cooked. Karen loves it. She talks about Gennie and Kenzie, about some online shopping, her brown eyes bright. I listen, my frustration from the day slowly receding. The quiet precision of my life, the intentionality, it reasserts itself in these moments. Jasmine sits in her little chair, occasionally reaching out, wanting to grab a piece of my steak. Karen laughs, catching her hand.

After Jasmine is tucked into her nursery, asleep for the night, Karen and I settle onto the long, deep couch. The big TV is on low, something we’re half-watching. She curls into my side, her hand finding my thigh, resting there. Her hair smells like her shampoo, and the warmth of her body against mine is a soft anchor. The day’s low-grade irritation, the interrupted gym flow, the hollow feeling of the MacDonald Highlands house – it all recedes into the background.

The lamp is low. Her breathing is slow and even. I feel her shift, her head resting on my shoulder. The quiet of the house, broken only by the distant hum of the AC and the almost-silent TV, settles around us. This is what I built. This is the quiet win. This moment, here, with her hand on my thigh, Jasmine asleep upstairs, the valley lights stretching out beyond our windows. It’s not loud. It doesn’t need to be. I know I’m the man now.
