The scent of freshly brewing coffee is the first thing that pulls me from sleep. Not an alarm. Never an alarm on Saturdays. Today is March 13, 2027. The light outside the blackout curtains is still soft, a pale grey hinting at the rising sun. Early March in Vegas holds a crispness, the air thin and cool even as the days stretch longer.

My side of the bed is already cool. Karen stirs beside me, a soft sigh escaping her lips. Her long balayage hair is fanned across the pillow, a dark root fading to honey blonde. She smells faintly of her perfume, a lingering sweetness from last night. Jasmine’s monitor on the bedside table is quiet. Good.

I move from the low platform bed, the hardwood cool under my bare feet. Every movement is deliberate, a quiet declaration of control. In the master bathroom, I splash cold water on my face, letting the shock chase the last tendrils of sleep. My reflection is sharp, jawline defined, eyes already focused. The dog chain necklace settles against my sternum, a familiar weight. It always starts here.

In the kitchen, the espresso machine hisses, pulling a rich, dark shot. I prefer drip on normal days, but today feels different. There's a hum beneath my skin, a quiet anticipation for an email, a message. It’s the annual target. The team has been on a tear. I know it’s coming.

Jasmine’s soft babbling drifts from the nursery. “Babababa… mamamama…” She’s just six months old, a few days past her half-year mark. Big strides. I finish my espresso, the dark, bitter liquid a clean line down my throat, then walk into her room.

The nursery is soft greens and creams, the morning light now spilling through the window, illuminating dust motes dancing in the air. Jasmine sits propped in her crib, a small, vibrant thing, pushing herself up with her arms, then letting herself fall back with a soft thump. Her dark eyes, Karen’s eyes, find mine immediately, and a wide, gummy smile splits her face. She looks more like Karen, but my coloring is starting to show, a hint of my own. I lift her, the small weight in my arms a different kind of strength, a quiet stillness. She coos, reaches a tiny hand for my nose. I let her grab it, her grip surprisingly firm.

Karen walks in then, stretching, her cropped set moving with her. Her smile, wide and easy, lights up the room. “Morning, my loves.” She takes Jasmine, presses a kiss to my cheek. “Coffee’s on.”

We drift into the kitchen, the smell of pancakes already starting to mingle with the lingering coffee. Karen is pulling out flour, eggs. “Did you see this email, Ryan?” she asks, gesturing to her phone on the counter. “My sister wants us to drive down to Henderson today, her son has a little league game. Mid-afternoon.”

I feel a small flicker of annoyance, a tightening in my chest. A calendar conflict I hadn’t seen coming. My own internal clock for Saturdays is precise: slow morning, an hour or two in the office, then a sunset drive with Karen. Not a drive to Henderson for a kid’s game. “Today?” I ask, the question flat.

She shrugs, flipping a pancake with practiced ease. “Yeah, it’s his first game. They really want us there.”

I lean against the counter, arms crossed. The espresso hits hard. This is the friction. The family rhythms, Karen’s family rhythms, sometimes clash with my own. I like the pace of her family, the warmth, the chaos of the Sunday dinners at her parents’ place, but I also like my own controlled quiet. Especially on a Saturday.

“Okay,” I say, finally. The word is measured. “What time?”

“Starts at three. We should leave by two, give us time.”

It pushes everything back. The office block I’d planned for after Jasmine’s morning nap, the quiet that allows me to focus on the numbers, the strategic pivots. It means the sunset drive would be rushed, or cut entirely. I don't respond, just nod. She reads it on my face. Karen’s smile softens, a small, knowing look. She doesn’t push. She just hums a soft, Spanish tune as she flips another pancake.

After breakfast, with Jasmine happily playing on her mat in the living room, babbling at the colorful shapes above her, I head to my office. The floor-to-ceiling windows offer a view of the still-pale valley. My solid wood desk is clean, the 49-inch ultrawide monitor dark. I power up the Mac mini, the faint hum a familiar sound. The first two hours of Saturday are typically sacred. Deep work. Reviewing the reports from the team, architecting the next moves.

I’m an architect here, not an operator. I see the lines, the flows, the slight adjustments that compound into bigger wins.

*“Always.”* I reply, then put the phone back down. He’s family. The visits are essential.

A slight rephrasing, a subtle shift in tone, and the conversion could jump by another percentage point. This is where I live now. The quiet, precise work of optimization.

I can push through another hour, maybe ninety minutes, then I need to shift gears. The frustration is still there, a low thrum beneath the surface. It is what it is.

Jasmine, now closer to seven months than six, is starting to show real preferences. She wants to be held, to explore.
